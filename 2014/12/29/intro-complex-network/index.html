A tree is an undirected simple graph that satisfy:

  • G is connected and has no cycles
  • A cycle is formed if any edge is added to G.
  • Not connected if any single edge is removed.
  • Any two vertices in G can be connected by a unique simple path.
  • 3-way Cayley Tree: leaves with k = 1, non-leaves with k = 3, average degree = 2, clustering coef. = 0.

###Network/Topological Properties

####1st Order Property

#####Degree Distribution
P(k) is the probability of a randomly choosen node has degree k. A histogram with x-axis presents k and y-axis presents P(k).

#####Path Length
A path is a sequence of nodes in which each node is adjacent to the next one. Note index starts with 0, and usually we only consider simple paths that do not have any cycles, no repeated nodes or edges.

P_n = {i_0,i_1,…,i_n}, P_n = {(i_0,i_1),(i_1,i_2),…,(i_n-1,i_n)}

Distances between a pair of nodes is defined as the number of edges along the shortest path connecting the nodes. If two nodes are disconnected, the distance is infinite. In directed graph, paths need to follow the direction.

Bread-First Search is used to finding shortest path.

Network Diameter is the maximum (shortest path) distance between any pair of nodes. Avg. distance for a connected graph/component is the average distance over all pairs of nodes.

####2nd Order Property
Properties based on the connectivity information of two nodes. Joint Degree Distribution P(k,k’) is the probability of k-degree node linking with a k’-degree node.

How many links? who connects with whom? We have two statistic projections: Degree correlation/Mixing pattern and Rich-club.

######Negative correlation - Disassortative mixing
Negative assortativity: nodes with high degree tend to connect with low-degree nodes.

####Centrality

#####Degree Centrality
A local measurement, interpreted in terms of the immediate risk of a node for catching whatever is flowing through the network (virus/information).

Mormalised degree Centrality = k/(N-1). N-1 is the max possible degree.
Freeman’s Network Centrality

#####Betweeness Centrality
Betweenness centrality is an indicator of a node’s in a network. It is equal to the number of shortest paths from all vertices to all others that pass through that node. A node with high betweenness has a large influence on the transfer of items through the network, under the assumption that item transfer follows the shortest paths.

Nodes of high betweenness centrality are important for transport. If they are blocked, transport becomes less efficient; if their capacity is improved transport becomes more efficient.

#####Closeness Centrality
n connected graphs there is a natural distance metric between all pairs of nodes, defined by the length of their shortest paths. The farness of a node s is defined as the sum of its distances to all other nodes, and its closeness is defined as the reciprocal of the farness. Thus, the more central a node is the lower its total distance to all other nodes. Closeness can be regarded as a measure of how long it will take to spread information from s to all other nodes sequentially.

#####Eigenvector Centrality (PageRank)
Eigenvetor centrality is ameasure of the influence of a node in a network. It assigns relative scores to all nodes in the network based on the concept that connections to high-scoring nodes contribute more to the score of the node in question than equal connections to low-scoring nodes. Page Rank and Katz Centrality are eigenvector measure.

####K-Core
The k-core of network can be obtained by recursively removing nofrd of degrees < k, until all remianing nodes have degrees >=k.

A node has the coreness value of c if it belogns the c-core but not to the (c+1)-core.

Nodes with the same coreness value of c form the c-shell of the network.

k-core Decomposition: Recursively prune the least connected nodes, reveals the hierarchical structure of a network, progressively focus on network’s central cores.

#####Power-law/Scale-free Network

The degree distribution is power-law after processing data.

Power-law degree exponents alpha. Typically 2 < alpha < 3

estimating alpha; bad for lit aline on log-log axis using least square method; good for ploting complimentary CDF, P(X>=x), on log-log axis. CDF is cumulative distribution function, much less noise, fit aline to obtain the slope alpha’ of P(X>=x), then alpha = alpha’ + 1

exponential: P(x) ~ f(x)=c^(-x)

power-law: P(x) ~ f(x)=c*(x)^(-alpha)

power-law is a line on log-log scale, exponential is aline on log-linear scale; power law is always higher than the exponential.

Power-law model has long tail.

network resilience, how the conncetiveity (path length) of netweok changes as teh nodes get removed randomly.

The average degree dose not characterise the scale of the network, waht matters is the power-law exponents.
